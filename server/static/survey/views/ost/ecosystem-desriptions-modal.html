Descriptions of Ecosystem Features

Following are definitions of the 10 Ecosystem Features, identified as the key habitat types and human uses in California MPAs. These definitions are for the purposes of MPA monitoring, and they reflect the jurisdictional limit of the Marine Life Protection Act. Click an Ecosystem Feature link to view its description.

Rocky Intertidal Ecosystems
Include areas of rock substrate occurring within the zone between mean high water and mean lower low water. This Ecosystem Feature includes exposed rocky cliffs, boulder rubble, exposed wave-cut platforms, and sheltered rocky shores.
Kelp and Shallow Rock Ecosystems (0-30m)
Encompass rocky substrates both with and without overlying kelp forests.
Mid depth Rock Ecosystems (30-100m)
Consist of rocky outcrops and pinnacles that range in depth from 30-100m.
Estuarine and Wetland Ecosystems
Encompass soft-sediment habitats, including tidal mudflats, eelgrass beds, and areas of open water. These systems have a shoreward boundary at the extent of tidal reach and salt-water-associated vegetation.
Soft-bottom Intertidal and Beach Ecosystems
Are wave-dominated areas of sand and gravel substrate occurring below mean high water and above mean lower low water.
Soft bottom Subtidal Ecosystems (0-100m)
Encompass the area of sediment substrate occurring at depths between mean lower low water and 100m.
Deep Ecosystems and Canyons (>100m)
Include both rocky and soft-bottom substrates in waters below 100m.
Nearshore Pelagic Ecosystems
Consist of the water column overlaying the continental shelf in state waters at depths greater than 30m.
Consumptive Uses
Encompass human use activities that involve the extraction of living marine resources. In the Central Coast region, this includes hand-collecting species on shore, by snorkeling, or by SCUBA diving and commercial and recreational fishing on shore or by boat.
Non-consumptive Uses
Include non-extractive shore-based and/or on-water recreational activities, including beach-going, diving, kayaking, board sports, and wildlife viewing.
